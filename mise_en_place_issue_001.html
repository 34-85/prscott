The Mise

This started, honestly, with a dinner. Friends coming over, and I needed wine — so I drove to the shop, and forty minutes later I was still there, talking grapes and bourbon and where to eat with people who love this stuff as much as I do. I drove home happy.

That's the whole idea. Mise en place is the cook's term for getting everything in its place before the heat comes on — and that's what this is: the weekly that gets you ready. The bottle worth seeking, the people behind the counter, what just landed on the shelf, and where to pour it. So the good life at your table is the easy part.

The Pour

It's July in Tennessee. Chill your reds.

The summer move nobody tells you about: light reds taste better cold. Twenty minutes in the fridge tames the alcohol, lifts the fruit, and turns a porch evening around. Ask your shop for a gamay (think Beaujolais), a Sicilian frappato, or a cool-climate pinot noir — low tannin, high refreshment, perfect with everything off the grill.

Tell the person behind the counter "something I can chill, under $25, for burgers." That sentence gets you a great bottle every time.

The Shop

Imbibe — the Southside shop that thinks like a restaurant

If you want to fall down a good rabbit hole, walk into Imbibe on the Southside (1616 Broad St). It's wine-led but built like a chef's pantry — a tasting bar with daily samples, a wall of beer on tap you can fill a growler from, and people behind the counter who'd rather ask what you're cooking than point at a shelf. It came out of Chattanooga's fine-dining world, and it shows.

The Maker

Bleu Fox — a cheesemonger from the very first class

Jesse and Brittany opened Bleu Fox Cheese Shop on East Main in 2017 — but the credential is the tell: Jesse earned his Certified Cheese Professional in 2012, in the program's inaugural class. That's a cheesemonger who was a pro before "cheesemonger" was a job title here. Grazing boards, 100-plus cheeses, charcuterie to build a night around. The name? Their kids — Amelia Bleu and Henry Fox.

The Table · drink well

Little Coyote, and the case for a frozen cocktail

Erik Niel's St. Elmo room earned a Michelin Bib Gourmand last fall, and the bar is half the reason to go: it leans agave, with a 25-page spirits list and a frozen "Texas Flood" — tequila, blood orange, Aperol, ancho chile. Niel's a two-time James Beard Best Chef: Southeast nominee, and his whole-animal butcher shop, Main Street Meats, made the Michelin list too. Order the frozen drink. Trust me. Host This · friends coming over?

The easiest win in entertaining is a board and three bottles, set out before anyone arrives — mise en place, made literal. One sparkling (it does the welcoming for you), one chillable red (see The Pour), and a three-cheese run from Bleu Fox: something soft, something sharp, something blue. Put it out, pour yourself one, and answer the door already relaxed. That's the whole trick.
