The First Word

Why we’re starting with whiskey

In February, Chattanooga Whiskey was named Best American Craft Producer at the World Whiskies Awards — the second time in four years this city’s distillery has taken a global title. Worth a toast. But the award isn’t why I’m starting here.

Distilling was illegal in Hamilton County from 1909 until 2013. A pre-Prohibition Chattanooga had more than 30 distilleries lining Market Street; then the law shut it all down for over a century. So when a couple of locals decided they wanted to make whiskey here again, they didn’t just open a business — they ran a grassroots “Vote Whiskey” campaign and helped change the state law. Then they built the country’s only standalone experimental distillery on the very street that used to be full of them.

That’s the entire spirit of this newsletter in one bottle: local people who saw something missing and built it back. Those are the stories we’re here for.

Meet a Maker

Mad Priest Coffee Roasters

Downtown · founded 2015 · Michael & Cherita Rice

Before they roasted a single bean in Chattanooga, Michael and Cherita Rice were working in coffee in India, where conversations with refugees from Syria, Iran, and Iraq reshaped what they wanted to build. Mad Priest opened in 2015 with a mission stitched into the business model: hire and train refugees resettling in Chattanooga, pay a living wage, and put the shops on bus lines so the jobs are actually reachable.

Their first employee, Tariq, came to Chattanooga from Sudan and helped clean and paint the place before it opened. The coffee’s genuinely good — but it’s the “why” behind the counter that keeps us coming back. This is what a local builder looks like.

Free or close to it. No agenda — just where we’d actually spend our time.

Wednesday · Main Street Farmers Market (Southside, by Finley Stadium, late afternoon). The city’s food-focused, producer-run market — go hungry, leave with dinner.

Open Tab

A few small things worth knowing.

A shop to know: Locals Only Gifts & Goods on Frazier stocks candles, hot sauce, pottery, and prints from 90+ Tennessee makers. Your one-stop “made nearby” gift run.

Civic-pride bite: the MoonPie was invented right here in 1917 and is still made by a family-owned Chattanooga bakery. A snack and a hometown legend.

Why “shop local” isn’t just a bumper sticker: studies peg about $0.68 of every dollar spent at a local independent staying in the community, versus roughly $0.43 at a chain. Where you spend is a small civic act.
