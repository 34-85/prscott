The Plate

Memo's chopped wiener, and the genius of never changing

There's a school of cooking that wins medals, and there's a school that just gets it exactly right, every time, for sixty years. Memo's Grill on East MLK is the second kind. The chopped-wiener plate — dogs under a thick, dark chili, slaw, mustard, onions, on a toasted bun with no business holding together but doing it anyway — is one of the great Chattanooga plates, full stop. Since 1966. No reinvention, no deconstruction; a single idea executed with the muscle memory of ten thousand lunches.

Somebody else in this town will tell you what the chili means. I'll tell you to beat the noon line and order two.

The Pour · my pick this week

I'm still chilling cru Beaujolais — Morgon, Fleurie, whatever your shop has cold. Gamay, lightly chilled, is the most food-friendly red on earth and the only thing I want next to a chili dog or anything off the grill. Ask Imbibe or Riverside for one under $25 and don't overthink it. No-proof table: a bone-dry cider does the same job — fruit, acid, fizz — and nobody at the cookout will blink.

The Counter · the craft behind it

The table starts at breakfast: a morning with Velo

Mise en Place is a table, and the table starts before the wine does. Velo Coffee has been roasting in Chattanooga long enough to be its own verb around here. Roasting is invisible craft until it isn't — too dark and it's ash, too light and it's grass; the good roaster lives in the ten-second window between, reading the crack of the bean by ear. Buy a bag of the house roast, grind it that morning, and you'll taste the difference between coffee and Coffee. That's the whole beat of this newsletter in one cup: someone got it exactly right, and most people walked past it.

From the Source · postcard from Kentucky

Spent the long weekend on the bourbon trail finishing the Executive Bourbon Steward course at Moonshine University in Louisville. Yes, it's a real credential; yes, the homework is drinking; yes, it's harder than that sounds. Full dispatch — and the three distilleries worth your own trip — next week. The one thing to steal today: at a tasting, add a few drops of water to a high-proof pour and watch it bloom. Costs nothing, changes everything.
